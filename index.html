          When to leave for the hospital

          Know the signs

          Early labor (usually OK to stay home)

          • Irregular contractions that come and go, more than 5–7 minutes apart
          • Mild cramping, low backache, "lightening" (baby drops)
          • Losing the mucus plug / bloody show (pink-tinged) — labor may still be days away
          • What to do: rest, eat light, hydrate, walk, time a few contractions, try to sleep

          How to time contractions

          Frequency = start of one contraction to the start of the next. Duration = start to end of a single contraction. Use the timer on the Hospital tab — it does the math for you.

          The 5-1-1 rule

          Contractions every 5 minutes, lasting 1 full minute, for 1 hour straight — call your OB and head in. First babies usually take their time, but always follow your own provider's instructions if they've given you different numbers.

          Call your OB right away if

          • Water breaks — note the time, color, and amount. Greenish/brown fluid: say so, it matters.
          • Regular contractions before 37 weeks
          • Bleeding more than light spotting
          • Baby is moving noticeably less than usual
          • Fever, severe headache, vision changes, or sudden swelling of face/hands
          ⚠ Go now / call 911
          • Heavy bleeding (like a period or more)
          • Severe, constant abdominal pain that doesn't ease between contractions
          • An overwhelming urge to push, or you can feel the baby coming
          • Something feels like it's in the vagina (possible cord) — get her on hands and knees, chest down, call 911
          • Seizure, fainting, or trouble breathing
          Logistics now, calm later: know your route + a backup route, where to park, and which entrance to use after hours. Keep the gas tank above half from week 36 on.

            What to expect, stage by stage

            1 · Triage / check-in

            Monitoring belts, a cervical check, lots of questions. If it's early, being sent home to labor more is normal, not a failure.

            2 · Early labor (0–6 cm)

            Often many hours, especially with a first baby. Walk the halls, hydrate, rest between waves. Your job: snacks, water, counter-pressure on her low back, calm voice.

            3 · Active labor (6–10 cm)

            Contractions get stronger and closer (every 3–5 min). This is the usual window for an epidural if she wants one — ask early, anesthesia can take time to arrive. Transition (8–10 cm) is the most intense part and often the shortest. If she says she can't do it, she's usually almost done.

            4 · Pushing & delivery

            Anywhere from 20 minutes to 3+ hours for a first baby. Nurses coach the timing. Your job: hold a leg if asked, stay at her head if not, keep telling her she's doing it.

            5 · The golden hour

            Right after birth: skin-to-skin on mom's chest, delayed cord clamping (you may be offered the cut), first latch attempt. Routine checks can usually happen ON mom — you can ask for that.

            6 · Recovery (24–48h vaginal, ~72–96h C-section)

            Baby gets a hearing screen, heel-prick blood test, bilirubin (jaundice) check, vitamin K shot, and eye ointment. Ask for the lactation consultant — more than once if needed. Nurses will check on mom often; let them, and let her sleep when she can.

                Feeding her

                How often

                Newborns eat 8–12 times per 24 hours — roughly every 2–3 hours, sometimes in clusters (especially evenings — normal). In the first days, wake her to feed if it's been ~3–4 hours, until she's back to birth weight and your pediatrician says feeding on demand is fine.

                Hunger cues (feed at the early ones)

                • Early: stirring, mouth opening, turning her head and rooting (searching with her mouth)
                • Active: stretching, more movement, hand to mouth, sucking on fists
                • Late: crying — a worked-up baby latches worse. Calm her first (skin-to-skin), then feed.

                Breastfeeding basics

                • First milk (colostrum) comes in tiny amounts — that's exactly what she needs. Mature milk arrives around day 2–5.
                • A good latch takes in more than the nipple — a big mouthful of breast. Persistent pain = ask for help, don't push through.
                • Offer both sides; next feed, start on the side you ended on.
                • Lactation consultants exist for exactly this. Use them early and often — it's a learned skill for both of them, not an instinct.

                Bottle basics

                • A newborn stomach is tiny: ~1–2 oz (30–60 ml) per feed in the early days, growing gradually. Follow her cues over the clock.
                • Paced feeding: hold her semi-upright, bottle near-horizontal, let her pause. Don't push her to finish.
                • If using formula: prepare it exactly per the label — never dilute or concentrate it.
                • Wash bottle parts thoroughly per the instructions. Fed is the goal, however she's fed.

                How you know she's getting enough

                • Diaper counts on track (table below) — the single best at-home signal
                • You can hear/see swallowing during feeds; she seems settled after most feeds
                • Weight: losing up to ~7–10% after birth is common; back to birth weight by ~10–14 days. The pediatrician tracks this — you don't need a scale at home.
                Ask the pediatrician about vitamin D drops if she's breastfed or partly breastfed — recommended for most breastfed babies, but get the product and amount from your doctor, not the internet.

                ⚠ Call your pediatrician if
                • Fewer wet diapers than days old (first week), or no wet diaper in 8+ hours
                • No stool in 48+ hours in the first week
                • White / pale-gray stool, or stool with blood (more than a streak)
                • Dry mouth, no tears when crying, sunken soft spot, unusually sleepy — possible dehydration

                Safe sleep (the ABC rule)

                Alone · on her Back · in a bare Crib — every sleep, naps included.

                • Back to sleep, every time, until she can roll both ways on her own
                • Firm, flat surface — crib, bassinet, or play yard that meets current safety standards
                • Nothing in the sleep space: no blankets, pillows, bumpers, stuffed animals, or positioners
                • Sleep sack or swaddle instead of blankets — stop swaddling at the first sign of rolling (often by ~8 weeks, earlier than you'd think)
                • Room-share — her sleep surface in your room — for at least the first 6 months, but not in your bed
                • Don't let her overheat: about one more layer than you'd wear; no hats indoors for sleep
                • No sleeping in car seats, swings, or loungers outside the car — if she conks out there, move her to a flat surface when you can
                • A pacifier at sleep time (once feeding is going well) is protective — and fine if she refuses it
                • Keep her environment smoke-free, always
                The 3am trap is the couch. If you're exhausted and might doze off holding her, an adult bed cleared of pillows and blankets is far less dangerous than falling asleep together on a sofa or armchair. Best of all: put her back on her own surface, even mid-protest.

                Warning signs — when to call

                ⚠ Fever — this one is non-negotiable
                • Rectal temp of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher in a baby under 3 months = call the pediatrician immediately, any hour of the night.
                • Don't give fever medicine first — under 3 months, that decision and the dose belong to the doctor.
                • A rectal thermometer is the accurate method at this age — have one before she's home.
                • Low temperature matters too: below ~97.0°F (36.1°C) and hard to keep warm — call.
                ⚠ Breathing — get help now
                • More than ~60 breaths per minute, sustained; grunting with each breath; nostrils flaring
                • Skin sucking in between or under the ribs with each breath (retractions)
                • Blue lips, tongue, or face — call 911
                • Pauses in breathing longer than ~10–15 seconds

                Jaundice (yellowing)

                Mild yellowing of the face and eyes is common around days 2–4 and usually peaks by day 5. It gets checked before discharge and again at the first pediatrician visit. Daylight near a window is the easiest place to look — press gently on her skin and see if it looks yellow when you let go.

                ⚠ Call if
                • Yellow is spreading down to the belly, arms, or legs
                • The whites of her eyes are deeply yellow
                • She's hard to wake, feeding poorly, unusually floppy, or has a high-pitched cry
                • Jaundice plus pale/white stools or dark urine

                Also call the pediatrician for

                • Vomiting that's forceful/projectile, green, or bloody — different from normal spit-up
                • Refusing 2+ feeds in a row, or too sleepy to feed
                • Inconsolable crying for hours, or a weak/unusual cry
                • Umbilical cord area red, swollen, oozing, or foul-smelling
                • Any rash with fever, blisters, or one that worries you
                • Anything where your gut says "this isn't right"
                Trust your gut. Pediatric offices run 24/7 nurse lines because new parents are supposed to call. No good doctor will ever make you feel silly for calling about a newborn. When in doubt, call — that's the whole rule.

                Soothing her

                The 5 S's (try them in order, stack them)

                • Swaddle — snug at the arms, loose at the hips
                • Side / stomach hold — in your arms only; she always sleeps on her back
                • Shush — white noise about as loud as a shower, near her ear
                • Swing — small, fast, gentle jiggles with her head supported. Never, ever shaking.
                • Suck — pacifier or a clean fingertip

                Also in the toolkit: skin-to-skin on your bare chest (works for dads too — a lot), motion (walking, babywearing), a warm bath, stepping outside, or simply a fresh diaper and a feed.

                Crying: what's normal

                Crying ramps up from ~2 weeks and peaks around 6–8 weeks — including stretches where nothing works and she isn't hungry, wet, or sick. That phase is well-documented, normal, and temporary. It is not a verdict on you.

                ⚠ Never shake a baby
                • If you hit your limit: put her down on her back in her crib, walk out, close the door, and breathe for 10 minutes. A safe baby crying alone is OK. Tag in your partner or call someone. Stepping away is the strong move, not the weak one.

                Umbilical cord care

                • Keep the stump clean and dry — air is the treatment. No alcohol rubs unless your pediatrician says otherwise.
                • Fold the diaper below the stump (or use newborn diapers with the cut-out notch)
                • Sponge baths only until it falls off — usually 1–3 weeks. Then tub time begins.
                • Let it fall off on its own — never pull, even when it's hanging by a thread
                • A few drops of blood or a little crust when it detaches: normal
                ⚠ Call if
                • Redness spreading on the skin around the stump
                • Pus, foul smell, active bleeding, or she cries when it's touched

                Good to know, week one

                • First pediatrician visit: typically 3–5 days after birth (1–2 days after discharge). Book it before leaving the hospital.
                • Baby girls: a little white discharge or even a tiny "mini period" of spotting in the first week is normal — mom's hormones leaving her system. Wipe gently, front to back, outside only.
                • Swollen breasts or genitals at birth — also hormones, also normal, resolves on its own.
                • Peeling skin, baby acne, flat pink patches ("stork bites") — normal newborn skin. No products needed.
                • Sneezing, hiccups, and irregular breathing rhythms (fast, then slow, brief pauses under ~10 seconds) are standard newborn behavior.
                • Nails: file or trim while she sleeps — newborn nails are sharp and she WILL scratch her face.
                • Visitors: washed hands, no kisses on her face or hands, nobody sick, nobody smoking. You're allowed to say no — "pediatrician's orders" is a free excuse.
                • Day/night confusion is standard issue. Bright and social by day, dark and boring at night. It recalibrates within a few weeks.

                Don't forget mom

                Her recovery is week one's other full-time patient.

                ⚠ For HER — get care fast if
                • Bleeding that soaks a pad in an hour, or clots bigger than an egg
                • Severe headache, vision changes, or new swelling — preeclampsia can start AFTER birth
                • Fever of 100.4°F+, chest pain, trouble breathing, or a painful/red/swollen leg
                • An incision or tear that's getting more painful, red, or oozing
                • Thoughts of harming herself or the baby, or she just "isn't herself" beyond passing baby blues — call her OB; the 988 line is there 24/7. Postpartum depression and anxiety are common, medical, and treatable — and they can hit dads too.

                Your highest-value plays this week: own the diapers and the burping, guard her sleep in real chunks, keep water and snacks within her arm's reach, run interference on visitors, and tell her — specifically — what she's doing well.
